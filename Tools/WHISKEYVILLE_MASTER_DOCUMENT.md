# PART I — THE VISION

## What Whiskeyville Is

Whiskeyville is a mobile tycoon/idle game set in 1933 Appalachian Tennessee,
the year Prohibition ended. You arrive at a ghost town with nothing but a cold
still and a deed to land nobody else wanted. You plant the first grain. You fire
the still for the first time in thirteen years. Slowly, deliberately, the town
breathes again.

It is the first whiskey game with genuine cultural soul. Not a bar management
game. Not a mixing simulator. The authentic American distillery process —
grain sourcing, mash bills, fermentation, copper distillation, char levels,
barrel aging, the rickhouse — is the mechanic AND the aesthetic. The gameplay
IS the craft. Players who finish a session of Whiskeyville know what a
rickhouse is. They know what Char #4 does to flavor. They look at a real
bottle of bourbon differently.

**The cultural moat:** No other game has this specific authenticity.
Whiskeyville owns post-Prohibition Appalachian distillery culture. That
territory is empty. This game claims it.

**The emotional hook:** To build back something again. This is not a game
about building an empire from scratch. It is a game about RESTORATION. The
town existed. It had life. Prohibition killed it. You are bringing it back.
That narrative is different from every other tycoon game, and it resonates
deeply with the Southern mindset — honoring what came before, rebuilding with
your hands, making something that will outlast you.

**The target player:**
- Primary: Whiskey enthusiasts, 25–45, who want a game that respects the craft
- Secondary: Cozy game players who want warmth, progression, and authenticity
- Tertiary: Southern culture fans, Americana lovers, Lynchburg/Jack Daniel's tourists
- Platform: Mobile (Android first, iOS second)

**The moat in one sentence:**
*Stardew Valley meets the authentic American whiskey tradition, set in a ghost
town you're bringing back to life.*

---

## The Philosophical Foundation

Three things make Whiskeyville different from every other mobile tycoon:

**1. The process is real.**
Every step of whiskey production in this game reflects what actually happens
in a distillery. Players don't just tap buildings — they understand why mash
becomes whiskey, why barrels matter, why char level affects flavor. The craft
is the game.

**2. The place has a soul.**
Whiskeyville existed before the player arrived. Old Pete remembers it. The
ruins of the buildings still stand. The player isn't building from empty land —
they are restoring something. This creates a fundamentally different emotional
relationship to the game world.

**3. The player IS the story.**
There is no named hero. The player names their own town, distillery, and
whiskey. When the sell notification reads "Rye-Forward Tennessee Bourbon ·
Ridge Road Reserve · Clara's Saloon," it is the player's whiskey, in the
player's town, sold through the player's relationships. The identity system
makes every playthrough personal.

# PART II — THE STORY

## Setting

The year is 1933. The place is a hollow in the hills of middle Tennessee,
two hours south of Nashville on a dirt road that most maps don't show.
The nearest town with a post office is Summerfield, population 340.

This hollow used to be called Whiskeyville. Not on any official map — just
what the locals called it, because of the distillery that sat at the center
of town and employed most of everyone in a thirty-mile radius. For forty
years the Whiskeyville Still produced what old-timers said was the finest
Tennessee whiskey in three counties. People came from Nashville to buy it.
Restaurants in Memphis served it by name.

Then 1920 came. The Volstead Act. The federal agents came through with axes
and padlocks. The still went cold. The workers scattered. The families moved
away in search of other work. Within two years the town was empty. Within
five years the weeds had started taking back the boardwalks. By 1933 the
buildings were still standing — Tennessee timber holds — but the town was
a ghost.

December 5th, 1933. The Twenty-First Amendment is ratified. Prohibition ends.

That's when you come.

---

## Act One — Cold Still (The Beginning)

You heard about the place from a man at a bus station in Knoxville. He was
old, hard-looking, said he used to work the cooperage there. Said the deed
to the whole parcel was sitting in a courthouse drawer, unclaimed, because
the original family had all either died or moved to Ohio. Said the land was
still rich. The creek still ran clean. Said if somebody was willing to work
for it, the town might come back.

You came.

The drive in takes forty minutes on a road that's barely a road. When you
come over the last ridge and see the hollow below you, it looks smaller than
you expected — and lonelier. Maybe a dozen buildings along what used to be
a main street. The saloon sign is still there, one nail, barely hanging.
The cooperage doors are swung open, the insides dark and dusty. And at the
center of everything: the distillery. Stone walls still solid. The copper
dome of the old pot still visible through a hole where the roof caved in.

You park. You get out. The only sound is wind and a creek somewhere in the
trees.

"Didn't think anybody'd ever come."

The voice comes from the porch of the old general store. An old man, seventy
if he's a day, in overalls and a hat that's seen too many seasons. He stands
up slow, like moving hurts.

"Name's Pete Calhoun. I've been here the whole time. Somebody had to keep
the rats from finishing it off."

This is where your story begins.

---

## Act Two — The First Grain (Weeks 1–3)

Old Pete doesn't waste words. He shows you where the grain storage used to
be — rotted now, but the foundation is good. He shows you the fields on the
south slope where corn grew every year for four decades. He shows you the
still house, tells you the pot still is bent but not broken, that a man with
patience and a decent fire could get it working again.

"The Calhoun family built this town," he says one evening, sitting on the
porch watching you work. "My father was head cooper here for thirty years.
My grandfather cleared the first field. When the law came through and shut
it down, it killed something in my father that never came back. He sat in
that chair over there for the last four years of his life and didn't speak
much." He's quiet for a moment. "I stayed because somebody had to remember
it was real."

You plant the first corn.

It takes longer than you expected. Everything does. But the day the first
harvest comes in — the smell of it, the weight of the grain in your hands —
something shifts. The still fires up for the first time in thirteen years.
The copper heats. The mash begins to bubble.

Old Pete stands at the still house door. He doesn't say anything. But he
takes off his hat.

---

## Act Three — Word Gets Around (Month 2)

Whiskey takes time. That's the thing most people don't understand. You can
rush grain. You can rush mash. But you cannot rush a barrel. The wood needs
to work on it. The char needs to open up the grain. It happens at its own
pace, and patience is not optional — it is the craft.

While the first barrels age, things begin to happen.

Clara Mae Hawkins shows up one morning with a wagon and a determined look.
She's heard the town is coming back. She wants to open a café — not a
restaurant, just somewhere people can sit down and have something decent.
She asks if there's a building she can use. You show her the old general
store. She looks at it for a long time. "I've worked with worse," she says,
and goes to work.

Within a week there's the smell of coffee and fresh bread in what used to
be a ghost town. The first time a stranger comes through — a traveling feed
salesman who took the wrong road — he smells the bread, follows the smell,
and sits down for an hour. He tells Clara Mae about your whiskey before he
leaves. He says he'll spread the word.

He does.

The first reputation comes not from the saloon but from the café. Clara Mae's
café becomes the social center of whatever Whiskeyville is becoming. People
start coming through just to see the place. They taste the whiskey at the
saloon. They talk about it when they get home.

Fame, the old-timers would have called it. Reputation. The kind of thing
you can't buy.

---

## Act Four — The Test (Month 3)

Not everyone is happy about Whiskeyville coming back.

Silas Drummond runs a competing operation in the next county. He's been
selling "medicinal spirits" through the dry years — never shut down, never
prosecuted, because he had friends in the right places. He's built a solid
business on being the only whiskey available in three counties. Now
Whiskeyville is cutting into his market, and Silas doesn't like it.

He comes through the first time with a friendly face. Shakes your hand.
Admires what you've built. Says he's glad to see the craft coming back.
Then, before he leaves, says something that lands different than it sounds:
"Real nice place you've got here. Shame what happened to the last people
who tried this."

He means the people who owned it before Prohibition. He's not making a
threat. He's making a reminder.

Sheriff Ray Pickett shows up around the same time. Ray is not a villain.
Ray has just seen a lot of people come through these hills with big plans
and big promises. He's watched towns try to come back and not make it.
He's cautious. He sits down, looks at your operation, asks clear questions.
What are you selling. Where are you selling it. Who's bought the land.
He's doing his job. By the time he leaves, he's satisfied. "I'll be back,"
he says, but it doesn't sound like a threat. It sounds like a man who
intends to keep an eye on something that might actually work.

The hardest test comes from an unexpected direction.

Temperance Bell is the daughter of the minister who used to preach in the
church on the edge of town — the same church that's now missing its bell.
She's forty, thin, precise in the way of someone who has organized her
grief into opinions. She doesn't come to you. She writes a letter to the
county paper. It says Whiskeyville is being rebuilt on the same foundation
that destroyed it the first time. It says the community learned a lesson
in 1920 and is forgetting it already.

The letter gets attention. Not all of it is bad attention. But it means
people are watching more closely now.

Old Pete, when you show him the letter, is quiet for a while. Then:
"Her father was my best friend. He drank himself to death in 1918, two
years before Prohibition passed. Died thinking the war was still on.
She's not wrong that liquor can kill a man. She's just wrong about
what kills him." He hands the letter back. "Make good whiskey. Let it
speak for itself."

---

## Act Five — Whiskeyville Returns (The Endgame)

You don't win Whiskeyville. You complete it.

There is a point — after the rickhouses are full, after the saloon is doing
real business, after Clara Mae has added a second table and a hired girl —
when Old Pete sits down on the step of the distillery and says something
you won't forget.

"My father used to say this town was built twice. Once by the men who first
cleared it, and once by the men who kept believing in it during the years
it was empty. He used to say the second building was harder, because you
had to believe in something that didn't exist yet." He looks at the street —
the lit windows, the smoke from the still house chimney, the new barrel
stacked outside the cooperage. "I think he was wrong. I think the second
building is easier. Because you've got proof."

The town is back.

Temperance Bell, in the final story beat, does something unexpected. She
comes in person. She sits in Clara Mae's café. She orders coffee. She doesn't
order whiskey. But she stays for two hours, and at the end she looks at you
and says: "He would have liked to see this, I think. My father. He loved
this place before it went wrong." She leaves without saying whether she
approves or disapproves. But she comes back the following week.

The sheriff tips his hat to you on the street and doesn't stop to ask
questions.

Silas Drummond, in the end, sends a case of his own whiskey with a note:
"I was wrong about you. Good work." Whether that means he respects you or
just knows he can't beat you — you decide.

The last screen of the story shows the town at dusk. Every building lit.
Smoke from the still house. The sound of fiddle music from the saloon.
Old Pete on the porch.

The text reads:
*"They said Whiskeyville was gone. You proved them wrong."*

# PART III — THE CHARACTERS

## Pete Calhoun (Old Pete)
**Role:** Tutorial guide, story witness, moral compass
**Age:** 72
**Appearance:** Weathered, lean, overalls, a battered felt hat he never removes indoors
**Voice:** Slow, Southern, never wastes a word

Pete Calhoun has lived in Whiskeyville for every one of his seventy-two years.
He was head cooper at the distillery before Prohibition — the best barrel man
in four counties, by his own modest accounting. When the agents came through in
1920, he watched them padlock the still house and felt something die that never
came back in its old shape.

He didn't leave because he didn't know where else to go. And then he stayed
because someone had to.

Pete is not a quest giver in any mechanical sense. He is a witness. He tells
you what was here. He gives the ruins context. His four story dialogue lines
are the spine of the tutorial experience:

- **Line 1** (first meeting): *"Didn't think anybody'd ever come. Name's Pete Calhoun.
  I've been here the whole time."*
- **Line 2** (after first Mash produced): *"That mash smells right. Your grandfather
  would've approved — if you had a grandfather in this business. Most of us did."*
- **Line 3** (after first sell): *"Folks are talking. Word travels fast in these hills.
  Faster now that Clara Mae's got a café for it to travel through."*
- **Line 4** (after Rickhouse built): *"Age it right and it won't just be whiskey.
  It'll be something people remember."*

**Development status:** ✅ Designed | 📋 Not yet implemented in code

---

## Clara Mae Hawkins
**Role:** Commerce hub, social nexus, secondary story character
**Age:** 38
**Appearance:** Strong hands, practical dress, always has flour somewhere on her
**Voice:** Warm, direct, takes no nonsense

Clara Mae arrived in Whiskeyville because she heard it was coming back and decided
to get there first. She's from Summerfield originally, ran a boarding house there
for seven years after her husband passed. She's practical, observant, and
constitutionally incapable of waiting for someone else to do something she can do herself.

She triggers at **Fame 100** — shows up with a wagon and asks if she can use the
old general store. Saying yes unlocks the General Store as a second sell channel.

Her role in the game: she functions as the social information system. Her
dialogue lines reference what's happening in the wider world — other towns,
other distilleries, what people are saying. She's also the first person to give
you a delivery quest (needing a specific whiskey for a specific occasion).

**Catchphrase:** *"I've worked with worse."*
**Signature quest:** "The Second Table" — bring Clara Mae 20 AgedWhiskey for
a county event. Reward: Tasting Room unlocked at Fame 500.

---

## Sheriff Ray Pickett
**Role:** Authority figure, skeptic who becomes an ally
**Age:** 55
**Appearance:** County sheriff uniform, badge, the look of a man who's seen everything twice
**Voice:** Measured, careful, always watching

Ray Pickett is not corrupt, not villainous, not a comedic obstacle. He is a
man doing a hard job carefully. He's been the sheriff for this county for
twenty-two years. He's watched people fail here, watched families leave,
watched towns die. He needs to see the evidence before he believes anything.

He first appears at **Fame 200** — stops by, asks clear questions, does his job.
He appears again at **Fame 500** — walks the whole operation, says almost nothing.
At **Fame 1000** — tips his hat on the street. That's the arc. No dramatic moment.
Just a man deciding, slowly, that you're the real thing.

His presence creates tension without conflict. He is the game's reminder that
what you're doing is real, consequential, and watched.

**Signature moment:** At Fame 1000, walking past the distillery, he stops,
looks up at the building, and says: *"My father said this town made the best
whiskey in Tennessee. I believe him now."* Walks on without waiting for a response.

---

## Elijah "Eli" Boone
**Role:** Traveling merchant, rare goods vendor, mystery figure
**Age:** Unknown (looks 50, may be older)
**Appearance:** Beat-up wagon, leather coat, always smoking a pipe he never lights
**Voice:** Friendly, elliptical, knows more than he says

Eli Boone shows up every few days at the edge of Zone 1. Nobody knows where he
comes from or where he's going. He trades in unusual things: rare seed varieties,
specialty barrel wood from different states, old distillery equipment he's found
in unlikely places. He never explains where he got anything.

Eli is the game's mystery character. He has no quest arc. He just appears, has
interesting things to sell or trade, and disappears. Players who pay attention
to his dialogue will notice he references Whiskeyville's original owners in ways
that suggest he knew them personally — but he never confirms this.

**Gameplay function:** Random merchant. Appears on a timer (every 3 real days).
Offers 2–4 rare items that rotate each visit. Items include: aged barrel wood
(adds Oaky flavor bonus), heirloom corn seed (produces higher-yield Corn batch),
old copper still fitting (reduces distillation time for one cycle).

**Catchphrase:** *"Found it. As I find most things."*

---

## Silas Drummond
**Role:** The rival. Not a villain — a competitor.
**Age:** 60
**Appearance:** Better dressed than you'd expect for the hills. Deliberate.
**Voice:** Friendly on the surface, careful underneath

Silas Drummond has been in the spirits business throughout Prohibition —
"medicinal purposes," of course, with winking connections to people in
county government. He's built a real business. He's not evil. He's just
someone whose comfortable monopoly is being disrupted by your success.

Silas appears at **Fame 300**, always with a friendly manner that conceals
watchfulness. His arc ends at **Fame 1500** when he sends the case of whiskey
with the concession note. He's the game's reminder that success has social
consequences — that doing something well means someone who was doing it badly
has to adjust.

He never does anything directly harmful to the player. His threat is always
implied. He represents the reality that building something real means competing
with people who've been comfortable in the space you're entering.

**His real function in the narrative:** Silas is the mirror. He shows what
Whiskeyville could have become — expedient, connected, survivable but soulless.
Your choice to do it the authentic way is contrasted against his way every time
he appears.

---

## Temperance Bell
**Role:** The conscience. The most complex character in the game.
**Age:** 43
**Appearance:** Precise clothes, careful posture, a face that's made peace with grief
**Voice:** Controlled, intelligent, not unkind

Temperance Bell's father, Reverend Josiah Bell, preached at the Whiskeyville
church for twenty-two years. He was also a functional alcoholic who hid it
behind scripture and self-discipline until he couldn't anymore. He died in 1921,
three months after Prohibition passed — as if the law removing the thing killing
him arrived too late to save him.

Temperance has never separated the town of Whiskeyville from what it took from
her family. Her letter to the county paper is not propaganda — it's honest grief
expressed as opinion. She is the most right character in the game about the
dangers of what you're building, and she is also ultimately capable of accepting
that a thing can cause harm and still be worth doing, if done with care.

Her arc is the longest and the most emotionally resonant:
- **First appearance:** The letter in the paper (Fame 250). You never meet her.
- **Second beat:** She passes through town, doesn't speak to you (Fame 600).
- **Third beat:** Clara Mae tells you Temperance sat in the café for an hour (Fame 1000).
- **Final beat:** She comes to you directly (Fame 1500). Sits down. Says her piece.
  The conversation is the player's choice: Listen or Justify. Both paths end with
  her leaving. But the Listen path leads to her returning. The Justify path does not.

**Why she matters:** Temperance Bell is the game's acknowledgment that the thing
you're building has weight. Whiskey has a history that includes harm alongside
craft. The game doesn't pretend otherwise. It just says: do it right, do it
honestly, let the quality speak, and even the harshest critics will eventually
sit down.

---

## Doc Alton Harper
**Role:** Support character, town doctor, voice of pragmatic optimism
**Age:** 58
**Appearance:** Doctor's bag always at hand, reading glasses he loses constantly
**Voice:** Warm, slightly distracted, fundamentally decent

Doc Harper was practicing medicine out of a converted barn in the next hollow
when word reached him that Whiskeyville was coming back. He relocated immediately.
He believes in towns. He's seen what happens to communities when they fragment —
he's been treating the dispersed, isolated families of the former Whiskeyville
area for years.

Harper's function in the game is warmth and grounding. He doesn't have a quest
arc. He appears at Fame 150 and stays. His building (the Medical Practice, upgraded
from a tent) unlocks a small game mechanic: Workers can occasionally become "fatigued"
(a minor slow debuff) and Harper's practice automatically resolves it. Not a major
mechanic — just a warm background presence.

His best dialogue line: *"A little whiskey never hurt anyone. Too much has killed
half my patients. The difference, as best I can tell, is the quality."*

---

## Hattie Suggs
**Role:** The town elder no one knew was still alive
**Age:** 88
**Appearance:** Tiny, wrapped in a quilt even in summer, sharper than she looks
**Voice:** The voice of someone who has survived everything

Hattie Suggs is a secret. She's been living in the back room of the old boarding
house since before the player arrived — Pete knows she's there but didn't mention
it because Hattie told him not to. She emerges at **Fame 400** when the town is
active enough that she can hear it from her window and decides the noise is
worth investigating.

Hattie was twelve years old when the distillery first opened. She remembers the
original whiskey. She remembers the original family. She is, in the game's
lore, the living link between what Whiskeyville was and what it is becoming.

Her dialogue is brief, rare, and always lands:
- *"The smell of mash in the morning. I thought I'd never smell that again."*
- *"My mother said this still was built by her grandfather. Stone by stone."*
- *"You make it taste like it used to and I'll die happy. That's not a small thing."*

Hattie gives one quest — the only one that's purely story-driven with no gameplay
reward: "Hattie's Request." She asks you to name one whiskey variant "The Old Way"
in honor of the original recipe. Completing it unlocks a commemorative bottle label.

# PART IV — HOW THE GAME WORKS

## The Core Loop (Systemic Description)

Whiskeyville is a **production chain tycoon** with **idle progression** and
**town restoration** as its meta-layer.

```
PRODUCTION CHAIN (what you do):
Plant grain → Harvest → Convert to Mash → Distill → Age in Barrel → Sell

META-LOOP (why you keep doing it):
Cash earned → Buy buildings → Expand production → Earn Fame → Unlock zones
→ Meet characters → Advance story → Prestige whiskey quality → Repeat at scale

RESTORATION LAYER (why it feels meaningful):
Each new building placed makes the ghost town more alive.
Each character who arrives gives the town a reason to exist.
The production chain is your argument that Whiskeyville deserves to come back.
```

## The Production Chain in Detail

**Stage 1 — Grain Sourcing**
- Grain fields are placed on tilled soil in the starting zone
- Fields grow automatically over time (idle — no tapping required)
- Different grains produce different flavor profiles when converted to mash
- Grain type is a strategic choice: Corn is abundant, Rye is potent, Barley is
  quality-focused, Wheat is mild
- Fields can be harvested with the Sickle tool when fully grown
- Each grain type has upgrade tiers that increase yield and quality

**Stage 2 — Mash Production**
- The Mash Tun accepts grain inputs according to its recipe list
- Multiple recipes run in priority order — first available recipe fires
- FlavorTracker accumulates flavor tags from grain type during mash production
- Mash is an intermediate resource — it doesn't sell, it converts
- The mash bill (recipe choice) is the first point of whiskey identity

**Stage 3 — Distillation**
- The Still converts Mash → Whiskey (white dog)
- Upgrade tiers increase output per batch and add quality bonuses
- Future: Column Still (Tier 2) allows higher throughput
- The still has a production sound (copper drip, steam) that plays each cycle

**Stage 4 — Barrel Making**
- The Cooperage converts Wood → Barrel
- Wood comes from trees on the map (axe tool) and future lumber operations
- Barrel quality affects aging outcome — char level is set at the Cooperage

**Stage 5 — Aging**
- The Rickhouse takes Barrel + Whiskey → AgedWhiskey
- This is the slowest step — intentionally
- Char level (set on barrel creation) affects flavor contribution
- Char #1 (Light): subtle wood notes
- Char #2 (Medium): balanced oak and vanilla
- Char #3 (Heavy): bold, smoky, deep amber color
- Char #4 (Alligator): maximum intensity, for advanced recipes
- FlavorTracker accumulates barrel flavor tags during aging

**Stage 6 — Selling**
- Saloon (first channel, always available): local market
- General Store (unlocks at Fame 100): broader reach, slightly higher price
- Tasting Room (unlocks at Fame 500): premium price, smaller volume
- Train Depot (unlocks at Fame 2000): regional distribution, large volume
- Export Dock (unlocks at Fame 5000): national/international, highest price
- Each sell generates Cash AND Fame
- The sell notification shows the full whiskey identity:
  [Quality] · [Distillery] · [Whiskey Name] · [Flavor Adjective] Bourbon · $X | +Y Fame

## The Flavor System

The flavor system is what makes Whiskeyville's whiskey feel real.

**FlavorTracker** accumulates tags across the production chain:
- Grain stage reports: grain's inherent flavor tags
- Mash stage reports: recipe's flavor contribution
- Barrel/aging stage reports: char level's flavor contribution
- On sell: top flavor tags determine the sell notification adjective

**Adjective mapping (examples):**
- Dominant Rye tags → "Rye-Forward Tennessee Bourbon"
- Dominant Corn tags → "Sweet Corn Heritage Reserve"
- Dominant fruit tags → "Strawberry-Infused Small Batch"
- Mixed grain tags → "Small-Batch Tennessee Blend"
- Heavy char + aged → "Barrel-Aged Single Malt"
- High quality all around → "Artisan Reserve"
- Maximum variety → "Heritage Blend"

**Why it matters:** The flavor adjective is the game's signature emotional
payoff moment. It tells the player their production choices MEANT something.
The whiskey they're selling has a personality derived from their decisions.
This is the Whiskeyville moat — no other mobile game does this.

## The Identity System

On first play, the player creates their whiskey identity through the
WhiskeyCreatorUI:

- **Town Name:** What the player calls their version of Whiskeyville
- **Distillery Name:** Their operation's brand
- **Whiskey Name:** Their core product's name
- **Mash Bill:** HighCorn (accessible, smooth), Balanced (all-rounder), HighRye (bold, complex)
- **Char Level:** 1–4, affects starting barrel char and flavor profile

These choices persist for the entire run and appear in every sell notification.
The player is always selling *their* whiskey, not generic whiskey.

On prestige (future feature): Player can update identity for the new run,
keeping a legacy board of previous whiskey names.

## The Economy

**Cash** is earned through selling AgedWhiskey. It is spent on:
- Building new production buildings
- Upgrading existing buildings
- Zone expansions (cash + Fame)
- Seed purchases
- Eli Boone's merchant goods

**Fame** is earned through selling AgedWhiskey (small amount per sale)
and through completing quests and story beats. It is the unlock currency for:
- New zones
- New sell channels
- Character arrivals (story triggers)
- Research unlocks (planned)

**Economy design philosophy:** The game never blocks a player from doing
the thing they love (producing and selling whiskey). There are no energy
timers. There are no gates that require premium currency. Progress is always
happening — if you're offline, your buildings are still running.

## The Zone System

The game world is a fixed 20×15 grid divided into concentric rectangular zones:

- **Zone 0 — The Homestead** (center, cols 6–13, rows 3–11): Free, unlocked at start.
  The original distillery footprint. Where the story begins.
- **Zone 1 — Back Hollow** (cols 3–16, rows 1–13): $500 + Fame 150.
  Expansion into the surrounding town area. Where the saloon, general store, and
  character buildings live.
- **Zone 2 — Ridge Road** (full grid, cols 0–19, rows 0–14): $1,200 + Fame 400.
  The outer edges. Where larger grain operations, the train depot, and future
  prestige content lives.

Locked zones are fogged. Fog clears dramatically when a zone is purchased —
a sweeping reveal animation with a music sting.

## The Tool System

Players interact with the world using tools, selected via HUD buttons or hotkeys:

| Tool | Key | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Hoe** | H | Till ground tiles for crop placement |
| **Sickle** | S | Harvest fully grown crops |
| **Axe** | X | Chop trees for Wood resource |
| **Demolish** | D | Remove buildings (confirm dialog) |
| **None** | (default) | Navigate, tap buildings/NPCs |

The charge mechanic: Hoe, Sickle, and Axe require holding the tap for a moment
before activating. This prevents accidental use and adds satisfying tactile feedback.
A visual ring appears at the touch point while charging.

## Idle Production

All production buildings run while the game is closed. This is essential for
mobile retention — players can check in, collect, and leave.

Offline calculation: on return, the game calculates how many production cycles
ran during downtime (capped at 4 hours to prevent excessive resource accumulation)
and delivers a "Welcome Back" summary:
*"While you were away: 12 Corn harvested · 6 Mash produced · 3 AgedWhiskey ready."*

## PROCESSING BUILDINGS

### Mash Tun ✅
**Purpose:** Converts grain → Mash. The heart of the production chain.
**Input:** Grain (recipe-dependent: Corn×2, Rye×2, Barley×2, or mixed)
**Output:** Mash (1 per batch)

**Recipes (priority order):**

**Upgrade Tier 1 (default):** 1 recipe slot active, standard speed
**Upgrade Tier 2 ($1,200):** 2 parallel recipes, +20% speed, Smooth flavor bonus

**Sound:** Low bubbling, periodic "glug" sound, steam exhale on batch complete

**Purpose:** Distills Mash → Whiskey (white dog)
**Input:** Mash (2 per batch)

# PART VIII — NPCs & QUESTS

## How NPCs Work (Technical)

NPCs in Whiskeyville are **scriptable dialogue objects** — no AI, no pathfinding,
no complex behavior trees. They are simple, warm, and easy to implement.

**The NpcBehavior system (planned):**
```csharp
// NpcBehavior.cs
// - NpcData SO contains: displayName, dialogueLines[], portrait, unlockFameRequirement
// - OnMouseDown() → opens DialoguePanel → cycles through dialogueLines[] in order
// - Each dialogue line can have: an optional flag to set in TutorialManager
// - NPCs don't move. They sit or stand at a fixed position in the world.
// - Tapping them is the only interaction.
```

NPCs are not autonomous agents. They are interactive set dressing with story
content. They don't wander. They don't do anything without player input.
This is the right choice for this game — the complexity is in the production
chain, not in simulated people.

**How they appear:**
- Each NPC has an `unlockFameRequirement` field
- When Fame reaches that level, the NPC GameObject activates in the scene
- A notification appears: "[Character name] has arrived in Whiskeyville"
- The NPC is immediately tappable

**NPC interaction flow:**
1. Player taps NPC in world
2. Dialogue panel slides up (portrait left, name, dialogue text, "Next →" button)
3. Lines cycle in sequence
4. On last line: panel closes. NPC enters "already spoken" state (shorter responses)
5. Some lines are gated by TutorialManager state (only appears after a condition is met)

# PART X — UI DESIGN BOOK

## Design Philosophy — Dopamine Architecture

Every interaction in Whiskeyville must feel good. Not just functional. Not just
clear. GOOD. The goal is that players physically enjoy touching the game.

**The 5 Dopamine Principles:**

**1. Every tap has a body.** Scale punch on every button press. The UI breathes.
Buttons don't just respond — they feel clicked.

**2. Every reward has a sound.** The collect sound, the cash sound, the level-up
sting — these are not optional. Sound doubles the dopamine response of a visual reward.
The player should feel the cash register even with eyes closed.

**3. Numbers move.** Resource counts don't jump — they tick up. A number going
from 5 to 12 should animate, digit by digit, not just appear.

**4. Progress is always visible.** The player should never have to wonder "is
anything happening?" Every building has a fill bar. Every offline session has a
collection screen. The game is always doing something.

**5. Unlock moments are cinematic.** Zone unfogs with a sweep and a music sting.
Character arrivals get a notification card that slides in elegantly. Tier 3 upgrades
play a burst of particles. These moments should feel like achievements even before
the achievement screen appears.

# PART XI — SOUND DESIGN

## The Audio Identity of Whiskeyville

Whiskeyville should sound like a living, breathing 1933 Tennessee hollow.
The ambient audio is the foundation. The UI sounds are the punctuation.
The music is the emotional current underneath everything.

## AMBIENT WORLD AUDIO (always playing, layered)

**Primary layer (always on):**
- Wind through pine and cedar
- Distant creek water over smooth stones
- Cicadas (day), crickets (night), frogs (evening)

**Secondary layer (day only):**
- Birdsong (mockingbird, cardinal, distant crow)
- Occasional distant train whistle (the sound of civilization, 2 miles away)
- Cow bell (far off — there's a farm somewhere)

**Secondary layer (night only):**
- Owl
- Fireflies (a high, soft shimmer sound — barely there)
- Cooler wind sound (pitch-shifted slightly)

**Production layer (based on active buildings):**
- Corn field: gentle rustle
- Mash Tun: low bubbling, occasional glug
- Still: copper drip, rhythmic tick, steam exhale
- Cooperage: muffled hammer, wood creak
- Rickhouse: ambient hum (aged whiskey has a sound — it's silence plus something)

### THE CULTURAL VISION

Whiskeyville is the first game in a space that has never been claimed:
**authentic American whiskey culture as a game genre.**

The Lynchburg tourism angle is real and sustainable. 300,000 people visit the Jack
Daniel's distillery every year. Many of them have never played a mobile game they
cared about. Many of them would carry this game home with them the way they carry
home a bottle — as an artifact of a culture that meant something.

The game's long-term potential is not just as entertainment. It is as **cultural
documentation** — a playable, living record of the craft, the people, the process,
and the specific American soul that produced one of the world's most recognized
spirits. That is not a small thing. It is, in fact, exactly the kind of thing that
lasts.

# PART XIV — DEV SPRINT GUIDE

## How This Game Gets Built

### The Session Structure

Every development session follows this protocol:

**1. READ THE CONTEXT DOCUMENT FIRST.**
Open `WHISKEYVILLE_CONTEXT.md`. Update the CURRENT GAME STATE section.
Do not write a single line of code until you know the current state.

**2. STATE THE SESSION GOAL.**
One sentence. "Tonight: single-page WhiskeyCreatorUI rewrite + SO creation."
Not "tonight: work on the game." Specific, completable, one task.

**3. WORK THE SMALLEST COMPLETABLE TASK.**
From the task list in the context document. One at a time. In order.
Never skip ahead. The floors must be stable.

**4. TEST WHAT YOU BUILT.**
In editor first. Then on device when possible. Every session should end with
something you can tap and see working.

**5. END WITH THE SESSION SUMMARY.**
What was completed. What is pending. What the next task is.
Update WHISKEYVILLE_CONTEXT.md accordingly.
Generate a Dev Bulletin and prepend to WhiskeyvilleBulletin.txt.

### The Minecraft Tower Rule

Before adding any new floor, ask: "Is the floor below this stable?"
Before building Old Pete, ask: "Does the production chain run end-to-end?"
Before building the cinematic, ask: "Does WhiskeyCreatorUI work?"
Before building the main menu flow, ask: "Is there a game for the menu to enter?"

### The Producer Rule

If a new feature idea comes up mid-session: acknowledge it, log it in
FUTURE FEATURES LOG, and return to the current task. The future features
log is where ideas are safe. In-session detours are where timelines die.

### Working With Claude

Claude is your senior programmer, producer, QA, and technical writer.
To get the best output:

1. Always paste `WHISKEYVILLE_CONTEXT.md` at the start of each new session
2. Update the CURRENT GAME STATE section before pasting
3. State the specific task clearly: "Write NpcBehavior.cs — OnMouseDown opens
   dialogue panel, cycles through NpcData.dialogueLines[], plays click SFX"
4. Review every script Claude writes before adding to Unity
5. Test before moving to the next task

The quality of output is directly proportional to the quality of context.
Vague prompts produce generic code. Specific context with current state produces
production-ready scripts that fit your architecture.

## FIRST LAUNCH

You tap the Whiskeyville icon. A loading screen appears — the ghost town at
dusk, warm amber light over darkened buildings, the logo fading in with a
letterpress feeling. Wind plays softly. A guitar begins, slow and melancholic.

The main menu arrives. Three options: BEGIN, CONTINUE (grayed — no save exists),
SETTINGS. You tap BEGIN.

The cinematic plays.

Nine images drift past — mist in the valleys, a dusty still, a ghost-town
main street, a headline that reads PROHIBITION REPEALED. A truck arriving at
a sign that says WHISKEYVILLE. Ruins of a stone distillery. A hand pulling on
a work glove. A vision of the town alive again. The title, amber on purple sky.

A voice says: *"They called it the dry years..."*

The music builds. The title holds. Fades to black.

---

**WHISKEY CREATOR PANEL**

A panel fills the screen. Warm parchment. An amber title: BEGIN YOUR LEGACY.

You are asked:
- *What do they call this land?* — You type: **Calhoun Hollow**
- *Name your operation.* — You type: **Ridge Road Distillery**
- *Name your whiskey.* — You type: **Cold Still Reserve**

You tap **Balanced** for your mash bill. The button glows amber.

The char slider sits at 2. You move it to 3. The label reads: *Char #3 — Heavy.*

The preview box updates in real time:
```
Town:       Calhoun Hollow
Distillery: Ridge Road Distillery
Whiskey:    Cold Still Reserve
Balanced Mash — Corn / Rye / Barley
Char #3 — Heavy
Est. Grade: B+
```

You tap BEGIN.

A satisfying collect sound. The panel fades.

---

**THE GAME WORLD — FIRST VIEW**

The camera opens on the town. A 20×15 grid. The center zone — the Homestead —
is clear. Rough terrain, stone foundations visible, a few trees. The outer ring
is fogged, dark, awaiting.

At the edge of the clear zone, a man stands on a porch. Something pulses gently
around him — an amber ring, barely visible. The game is saying: *tap this.*

You tap Pete Calhoun.

A dialogue panel slides up. An old man's portrait, weathered and warm.

*"Didn't think anybody'd ever come. Name's Pete Calhoun. I've been here the whole
time. Somebody had to keep the rats from finishing it off."*

[Next →]

*"The distillery was right here at the center. My father worked the cooperage for

needs attention but it's not gone."*

[Next →]

*"Start with the fields. Everything begins there."*

[Close]

A quest notification slides in from the right: **"Cold Still" — Place your first
Corn Field.**

**FIRST BUILD**

The HUD shows: **$200 Cash | Fame: 0**

You tap the BUILD button (bottom left). The Build Panel slides up — wooden frame,
amber accents. A Corn Field card sits at the top left: **$50**.

You tap the Corn Field card. A highlight appears at the bottom: *Corn Field needs
tilled soil.*

The Hoe tool icon appears in the corner. You tap it. Your cursor changes.

You tap a tile in the center zone. The tile darkens — tilled. You tap a few more.
Satisfying soil sounds each time.

You select the Corn Field in the Build Panel. You tap a tilled tile.

**THUD.** A building pops into existence with a satisfying scale-punch animation.
A floating **+Corn Field** text drifts upward.

A thin amber progress bar appears beneath the building. It begins to fill.

---

**FIRST HARVEST**

8 seconds pass. The progress bar reaches full and pulses.

**+2 CORN** floats up in yellow text.

The bar resets and begins filling again.

Your HUD shows: Corn: 2.

You place two more Corn Fields. Three fields now, each with their own progress bars,
cycling at slightly different rates (staggered from when you placed them). The world
is starting to feel like something is happening.

---

**BUILDING THE CHAIN**

You build the Mash Tun ($200). A production building, stone and copper, bubbling
slowly. Its bar waits — it needs 2 Corn before it can run.

6 more seconds. You have 4 Corn.

The Mash Tun fires. Low bubbling sound. The bar fills faster than the Corn Fields —
5 seconds. **+1 MASH** floats up in orange text.

You build the Still ($350). Wire-looking copper contraption. It needs Mash.

You already have 2 Mash waiting (accumulated while you were placing the Still).
The Still fires immediately. Copper drip sound, rhythmic. **+1 WHISKEY** in white.

You spend your remaining cash ($650 left) on the Cooperage ($150). You need Wood.

An Oak Tree stands in the northeast corner of the Homestead. You select the Axe

falls with a satisfying crack. **+3 WOOD** drifts up.

You use the last $500 to buy the Rickhouse. It needs a Barrel AND a Whiskey.
You have both.

The Rickhouse fires. Deep ambient hum begins. The aging shimmer plays.
30 seconds. The longest wait yet. But a progress bar is filling.

30 seconds later: **+1 AGED WHISKEY** in gold.

You have AgedWhiskey.
